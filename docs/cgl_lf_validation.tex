\begin{abstract}
Pressure anisotropy may reorganize collisionless, high-\(\beta\) turbulence
before microscale instabilities provide an effective collisional closure, but
the numerical evidence for this ``magneto-immutable'' response is demanding
to reproduce.  We define an independent AthenaK reproduction of the
CGL--Landau-fluid (CGL--LF) simulations of Majeski, Kunz, and Squire (2024,
MKS24), after validating the heat-flux operator, threshold policy, driven
turbulence problem generator, and retained diagnostics.  The reproduction
targets sixteen unique source-mapped CGL--LF calculations spanning
active/passive pressure feedback, forcing geometry, beta, heat-flux scale,
limiter scattering, and scale separation.  Several dimensionless published
products admit checksum-qualified digitized comparisons, whereas absolute
spectral ordinates remain blocked pending a documented transform or original
numeric data; the hybrid-kinetic comparison is outside a direct CGL--LF
recalculation.  The production campaign has commenced, but no
standard-duration comparison is claimed in this version of the manuscript.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

In a weakly collisional plasma, conservation of the adiabatic invariants can
make changes in magnetic-field strength energetically expensive: field
amplification and reduction generate a pressure anisotropy that acts back on
the turbulent strain.  A central question is whether this feedback suppresses
the field-strength-changing motions before mirror and firehose regulation
dominates the dynamics.  MKS24 addressed this question with driven
high-\(\beta\) CGL--LF turbulence and found strong active/passive contrasts in
instability exposure, transfer, and scale-dependent organization.

The numerical claim is consequential because it combines non-local
heat-flux closure, threshold-activated anomalous scattering, and turbulent
statistics in an elongated mean-field box.  An independent reproduction must
therefore match the physical experiment before interpreting a new parameter
scan.  In particular, MKS24 varies the anomalous limiter rate after a
microinstability threshold is crossed; it does not scan a uniform background
collision frequency.  We retain the latter experiment as later work, rather
than confusing it with the published target.

This manuscript integrates the illustrated validation report originally
maintained on branch \texttt{CGL-STS-LF} into the current
\texttt{feature/cgl-landau-fluid} implementation and establishes the
reproduction campaign.  It documents the AthenaK CGL--LF method, preserves
method-level validation figures, states the MKS24 comparison contract, and
records the production and reference-data boundaries needed for defensible
claims.

The method-level results show that the implemented update follows the intended
finite-volume variable strategy and passes checks including a
one-dimensional oblique linear initial-value problem using the Squire et
al.\ Figure 17 background state.  The reduced nonlinear figures exercise
analysis and plotting paths only: they are not standard-resolution,
steady-state, or quantitative MKS24 reproduction results.  The current
execution and reference-admission status is maintained in
\texttt{docs/cgl\_lf\_mks24\_reproduction\_implementation\_plan.md} and in
the Sphinx CGL--LF validation guide.

\section{MKS24 Target Contract}

The directly reproducible target is the CGL--LF simulation component of MKS24.
We use a fully periodic domain
\([L_x,L_y,L_z]=[1,1,2]\), with the background magnetic field along \(z\),
standard resolution \(192\times192\times384\), and initial
\(\beta_0\in\{1,10,100\}\) where required by the mapped published panels.
The nominal closure scale is
\(\lvert k_\parallel\rvert=4\pi/L_\parallel=2\pi\), the background collision
frequency is zero, and the standard microinstability treatment is a hard-wall
limiter at the mirror and parallel-firehose thresholds,
\(\beta\Delta=1\) and \(\beta\Delta=-2\).  The forced calculations use an
Ornstein--Uhlenbeck velocity source with fiducial injection
\(\dot E_K=0.32\rho_0v_A^2L_\perp^3\), shell
\(k/(2\pi/L_\parallel)\in[1,3]\), and power proportional to \(k^{-2}\).
Every production calculation must reach at least
\(t_f=10L_\perp/v_A\); comparisons use declared late-time windows no shorter
than \(2L_\perp/v_A\).

The comparison has two deliberate limitations.  First, MKS24 Figure 10 uses
a hybrid-kinetic \texttt{Pegasus++} calculation and is external context unless
that separate dataset or an equivalent kinetic rerun is obtained.  Second,
dimensionful spectral ordinate comparisons are withheld where the published
material does not state the discrete Fourier normalization required for an
AthenaK transform.  We may therefore establish reproduction of specified
CGL--LF products, but we do not claim complete reproduction of every published
panel unless these boundaries are closed.

\section{Conserved Variables}

The hyperbolic CGL system evolves total energy and the conserved anisotropy
variable
\begin{equation}
  A = \rho \log\left[
    \frac{p_\perp}{p_\parallel}
    \frac{\rho^2}{|B|^3}
  \right].
\end{equation}
The legacy array slot is still named \texttt{IMU}, with \texttt{IAN} used as an
alias in CGL code.  The Riemann solve remains in \(A\), which avoids the
small-\(|B|\) pathologies associated with evolving magnetic moment directly.

The LF STS operator temporarily changes only the interpretation of the
\texttt{IAN} slot:
\begin{equation}
  A \rightarrow \mu_B = \frac{p_\perp}{|B|}.
\end{equation}
The standard conserved-variable STS update then advances \((E,\mu_B)\) with the
LF flux divergence.  Immediately after the RKL2 stage, the EOS converts
\(\mu_B \rightarrow A\), and the normal CGL conserved-to-primitive recovery
restores \((p_\parallel,p_\perp)\).

In the current source tree the LF parabolic operator is owned by
\path{src/diffusion/cgl_landau_fluid.*}; the CGL state and constraint
logic are implemented in \path{src/eos/cgl_mhd.cpp} and
\path{src/eos/cgl_physics.hpp}.  The quantitative method tests use
\path{src/pgen/tests/cgl_landau_fluid.cpp}, while the reduced MKS24
initialization and history diagnostics use
\path{src/pgen/tests/cgl_lf_paper.cpp}.

\section{Landau-Fluid Closure}

The implemented closure uses the Snyder--Hammett--Dorland 3+1 heat fluxes,
\begin{align}
  q_\parallel &=
    -\chi_\parallel \rho \nabla_\parallel\left(\frac{p_\parallel}{\rho}\right),
  \\
  q_\perp &=
    -\chi_\perp \left[
      \rho \nabla_\parallel\left(\frac{p_\perp}{\rho}\right)
      - p_\perp\left(1-\frac{p_\perp}{p_\parallel}\right)
        \frac{\nabla_\parallel |B|}{|B|}
    \right],
\end{align}
with the finite-collision Squire et al. coefficients
\begin{equation}
  \chi_\perp =
    \frac{2c_\parallel^2}
      {\sqrt{2\pi}\,c_\parallel |k_\parallel| + \nu_{\rm eff}},
  \qquad
  \chi_\parallel =
    \frac{8c_\parallel^2}
      {\sqrt{8\pi}\,c_\parallel |k_\parallel|
        + (3\pi-8)\nu_{\rm eff}} .
\end{equation}
Here \(\nu_{\rm eff}\) is the background collision frequency plus any active
mirror/firehose limiter scattering at the face.  In the collisionless limit,
these reduce to
\(\chi_\perp=\sqrt{2/\pi}\,c_\parallel/|k_\parallel|\) and
\(\chi_\parallel=\sqrt{8/\pi}\,c_\parallel/|k_\parallel|\).
Here \texttt{lf\_k\_parallel} is the closure wavenumber \(|k_\parallel|\), not a
conductivity.  In local mode, \(c_\parallel=\sqrt{p_\parallel/\rho}\) is computed
from the live face state.  In background mode, only this prefactor is frozen to
\texttt{lf\_c\_parallel0}; all gradients and thermodynamic variables remain live.

The face fluxes coupled into STS are
\begin{equation}
  F_E^i = \hat{b}^i \left(q_\perp + \frac{1}{2}q_\parallel\right),
  \qquad
  F_{\mu_B}^i = \hat{b}^i \frac{q_\perp}{|B|}.
\end{equation}

\section{Heat-Flux Cap}

Following Squire et al. (2023), equations 3.1--3.2, the unlimited heat flux
\(q_L\) is capped as
\begin{equation}
  q = \frac{q_L q_{\max}}{q_{\max} + |q_L|}.
\end{equation}
The implemented caps are
\begin{equation}
  q_{\perp,\max} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} c_\parallel p_\perp,
  \qquad
  q_{\parallel,\max} = \sqrt{\frac{8}{\pi}} c_\parallel p_\parallel.
\end{equation}
The cap is applied after computing the unlimited flux with the collision-aware
coefficient, so collisions and limiter scattering suppress the flux before the
smooth free-streaming bound is applied.

\section{Threshold Policy and Retained Diagnostics}

The current implementation separates backward-compatible operator tests from
MKS24 production definitions.  Existing method checks retain the default
oblique-firehose policy.  MKS24 decks set
\texttt{cgl\_firehose\_threshold = parallel}, selecting
\(\beta\Delta \leq -2\), together with the mirror threshold
\(\beta\Delta \geq 1\).  Hard-wall MKS24 decks additionally set
\texttt{limiter\_hardwall = true}; newly unstable primitive states are then
projected to the selected threshold while preserving
\(p_\perp + p_\parallel/2\), rather than relying on finite-rate limiter
relaxation to approximate an algebraic wall.

LF-enabled runs append persistent history counters for STS stages, floors,
non-finite and non-positive states, ordinary limiter occupancy, emergency
hard-bound crossings, heat-flux-cap activity, applied heat-flux
contractions, and hard-wall projection applications.  When enabled for
paper decks, \texttt{cgl\_lf\_record\_pressure\_work} also retains
explicit-RK-applied total and anisotropic CGL pressure-traction work.  Strict
validation rejects floors, invalid states, or emergency-bound crossings;
hard-wall projection activity is an expected model action, not by itself a
safety failure.

\section{Figure 17-Style Linear Initial-Value Check}

The paper-style inputs use the Figure 17 background
\begin{equation}
  \rho_0 = 1,\quad p_{\parallel 0}=p_{\perp 0}=5,\quad
  u_0=0,\quad B_0=(1,\sqrt{2},0.5),\quad k=2\pi \hat{x},
  \quad kL=|k|.
\end{equation}
The test initializes a small transverse velocity perturbation, not a CGL
eigenmode, and compares the
final complex Fourier amplitudes of \(u_y\), \(B_y\), \(p_\parallel\), and
\(p_\perp\) against an offline linearized CGL--LF initial-value reference integrated with the
same background state and closure coefficients.  Two inputs are provided: one
with the LF heat flux enabled and one with pure CGL.  This checks the same
method class as the Appendix A.2/Figure 17 linear-wave convergence problem.

\section{Figure 17-Style Eigenmode Checks}

The validation suite now also includes exact eigenmode initializations for the
Alfvén, slow-like, and fast-like positive-frequency branches of the same
linearized Figure 17 system.  The script
\texttt{scripts/generate\_cgl\_lf\_eigenmode\_inputs.py} constructs the
linear CGL or CGL--LF matrix for the AthenaK variables
\((\rho,u_x,u_y,u_z,B_y,B_z,p_\parallel,p_\perp)\), diagonalizes it, normalizes
the selected eigenvectors, and writes their complex amplitudes and eigenvalues
into AthenaK input files.  The pgen initializes
\(\mathrm{Re}[\delta U_k \exp(ikx)]\) and compares the final Fourier mode with
\(\delta U_k\exp(\lambda t)\), where \(\lambda\) is the supplied eigenvalue.
These inputs are the direct replacement for using a single transverse-velocity
seed as a proxy for the Squire/Jono linear-wave comparisons.

\section{MKS24 Reproduction Boundary}

The current branch defines guarded standard, limiter-frequency, heat-flux,
compressive, and scale-separation workflows and supports
checksum/uncertainty-qualified external reference comparisons.  The admitted
published reference products presently include Figure 2(a) transformed
sampled surfaces, Figure 2(b) histories, normalized Figure 4(a) PDFs,
normalized Figure 5(b) eddy scales, dimensionless Figure 7 transfer ratios,
Figure 8 selected-shell alignment PDFs, Figure 9 and Figure 11/12 alignment
curves, and Figure 13(b),(d) dimensionless products.  Because each admitted
panel product retains its own provenance manifest, an accepted campaign
bundle is analyzed by repeating \texttt{paper-analyze --reference-curves}
for the admitted manifests; the analyzer records every manifest checksum and
provenance block and rejects duplicate product identifiers across manifests.

Remaining absolute spectral or strain ordinate panels are intentionally not
admitted: the paper and cited donor source define shell spectra and plotted
units but do not state the discrete Fourier normalization needed to map
absolute digitized ordinates into AthenaK products.  Those panels require
author/archive numeric data, donor diagnostic code, or an explicit
normalization statement.  One mapped production segment has been retained
only as clean partial I/O-diagnostic evidence; the ranked-output replacement
is accepted through its \(t=2\) prefix and is being continued toward the
required \(t=10\) final time.  Its transient-prefix bundle has verified the
rank-local assembly and diagnostic path, but the production comparison window
is \(8\leq t\leq10\).  Quantitative MKS24
simulation-to-reference comparisons require completed accepted bundles; none
is claimed by this manuscript version.

\section{Discussion}

The reproduction is designed to distinguish a physical active-pressure
response from two easier but weaker demonstrations: a correct implementation
of individual LF terms and a visually plausible short turbulence transient.
The validation results establish the first requirement at the operator and
linear-wave levels.  The guarded Stage I matrix and the comparison gates make
the second requirement testable, but it cannot be resolved by the reduced
diagnostic bundles shown above.  Evidence for the MKS24 interpretation will
require late-time active/passive contrasts in the admitted instability,
transfer, alignment, and sensitivity products from accepted production data.

Two limitations are structural rather than statistical.  The spectral
ordinate panels cannot be used for absolute residual tests until the
published normalization is mapped to the AthenaK transform or original
numeric products are recovered.  Similarly, no CGL--LF rerun is an
independent reproduction of the hybrid-kinetic Figure 10 comparator.  These
limitations narrow the claims supported by Stage I but do not prevent
quantitative evaluation of the admitted CGL--LF diagnostics.

The later weak-guide-field and background-collisionality study is therefore
not part of the present results.  In particular, varying uniform background
collisionality would answer a different question from changing the
threshold-activated limiter rate in MKS24.  Treating those scans as
interchangeable before the reproduction baseline is accepted would obscure
both the numerical and physical comparison.

\section{Conclusions}

AthenaK now has a validated CGL--LF implementation path, retained nonlinear
diagnostics, source-mapped MKS24 case definitions, and a checksum- and
allocation-recorded Frontier production protocol.  The production protocol
began with the required low-resolution scale-separation case; its first
shared-I/O segment is a clean partial diagnostic record, and its ranked-output
replacement has passed the inspected \(t=2\) prefix gate and is continuing
toward \(t=10\).  No completed case is yet counted as an accepted comparison
result.  Thus the present manuscript establishes the method, target contract,
and acceptance procedure, but does not yet claim reproduction of the MKS24
turbulent conclusions.

Completion of the reproduction claim requires all sixteen mapped cases to
reach \(t_f\geq10L_\perp/v_A\), pass strict safety and provenance checks, and
be evaluated against each admitted comparison gate.  Results of the later
extension campaign will be reported only after that baseline is complete and
a separate execution plan is approved.
