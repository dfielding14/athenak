\title{Prescribed-Velocity Scalar Mixing in AthenaK:\\
Frozen-Hydrodynamic Transport and Spectral Velocity Generation}

\begin{abstract}
Prescribed-velocity scalar mixing is only as well posed numerically as the two operators that define it. The first is the frozen-hydrodynamic transport operator that advances passive scalars while holding the background density, momentum, and energy fixed. The second is the velocity generator that supplies the prescribed flow while respecting the desired solenoidal structure and shell-integrated power spectrum. In AthenaK both ingredients are now implemented explicitly in the \code{scalar\_mixing} problem, but they resolve different uncertainties. The hydro-side uncertainty is whether \SO is in fact equivalent to \code{time/evolution=kinematic}; it is not. The spectral uncertainty is how to realize the same target velocity spectrum with either projection-based or stream-based constructions in two and three dimensions. In this note we document both operators in the form actually executed by the code. We show that \SO freezes the built-in evolution of the hydro conserved state while still transporting each scalar conservatively with the hydro face mass flux and optional explicit scalar diffusion. We then derive the four implemented divergence-free velocity generators: 2D projection, 2D stream function, 3D projection, and 3D Clebsch stream initialization with $\mathbf{u}=\nabla \phi_1 \times \nabla \phi_2$. The common control parameters are the driving band $\left[k_{\min},k_{\max}\right]$, the target shell-integrated slope $E_u(k)\propto k^{-\mathrm{expo}}$, the rms normalization, and the retained-mode importance sampling above \code{turb\_k\_crit}. A dedicated diagnostics export now records the retained mode catalogs, coefficients, latent stream fields, and fitted 3D shell weights. In the reference campaign presented here the initialized fields match the target rms speed to better than $10^{-10}$ relative accuracy, remain divergence-free at the level of ${\sim}10^{-7}$ in the spectral norm, and recover the requested $-5/3$ velocity slope to good accuracy in all four modes, while the 3D stream construction no longer exhibits the pathological high-$k$ spike that motivated the present rewrite.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Prescribed-velocity scalar transport is attractive because it isolates a physically clean uncertainty. If the objective is to understand how a passive scalar responds to a chosen flow, then one would like the scalar transport operator to be conservative and stable, while the prescribed velocity itself should realize a well defined spectrum, geometry, and amplitude. In practice those two requirements are easy to conflate. A user can specify a velocity field that is divergence-free in the continuum sense and still obtain the wrong discrete transport problem if the hydro variables are not actually frozen. Conversely, one can freeze the hydro update but still generate a velocity field whose shell-integrated spectrum differs from the slope encoded in the input file.

The present \code{scalar\_mixing} implementation in AthenaK addresses both issues, but they live in different parts of the algorithm. On the hydro side, \SO defines a frozen-background scalar operator that is distinct from \kin. On the problem-generator side, the user may now choose between projection-based and stream-based solenoidal velocity initialization through \code{problem/turb\_use\_stream\_function}. In two dimensions the stream construction is the familiar
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{u} = \left(\partial_y \psi,\,-\partial_x \psi,\,0\right),
\end{equation}
whereas in three dimensions it is the Clebsch form
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{u} = \nabla \phi_1 \times \nabla \phi_2.
\end{equation}
These choices do not merely decorate the same random field. They determine which latent scalar potentials exist, how the velocity coefficients are assembled, and what shell-amplitude law is needed to realize a target velocity spectrum.

The central purpose of this note is therefore to remove two different ambiguities. The first is the discrete meaning of frozen-hydrodynamic scalar transport. The second is the spectral meaning of the user inputs \code{turb\_nlow}, \code{turb\_nhigh}, \code{turb\_expo}, \code{turb\_v\_rms}, and \code{turb\_k\_crit} across all four implemented velocity-generation modes. We proceed in that order. Section~\ref{sec:transport} compresses the hydro-side operator to the essential distinction between \SO and \kin. Section~\ref{sec:spectral-framework} introduces the common spectral framework. Sections~\ref{sec:2d} and \ref{sec:3d} derive the two-dimensional and three-dimensional generators. Section~\ref{sec:validation} validates the resulting fields and the reproducibility-facing diagnostics export. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} closes with practical recommendations for users. An appendix then provides a per-mode diagnostic atlas.

\section{Frozen-Hydrodynamic Scalar Transport}
\label{sec:transport}
The first uncertainty this note resolves is whether AthenaK already contains a coherent discrete operator for prescribed-velocity scalar transport. It does, but it is \SO rather than \kin. The distinction matters because the passive scalars are stored in conservative form,
\begin{equation}
q_n \equiv \rho s_n,
\end{equation}
and therefore inherit whatever face mass flux the hydro subsystem actually computes.

\subsection{What \texorpdfstring{$\SO$}{scalar\_only} freezes}
Let
\begin{equation}
U_h \equiv \left(\rho,\rho v_x,\rho v_y,\rho v_z,E\right)
\end{equation}
denote the hydro conserved state. The Runge--Kutta stage update has the schematic form
\begin{equation}
U^{(\ell)} = \gamma_0 U^n + \gamma_1 U^{(\ell-1)}
  - \beta_\ell \Delta t \nabla\cdot F^{(\ell-1)}
  + \beta_\ell \Delta t S^{(\ell-1)}.
\end{equation}
Under \SO, AthenaK still reconstructs the primitive state, still solves the chosen hydro Riemann problem, and still computes the face mass flux $F_\rho$. What changes is the index range of the conservative update: the hydro loop begins at the first scalar component instead of at density. Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
U_h^{(\ell)} = U_h^n,
\end{equation}
while the passive scalars continue to receive their conservative flux-divergence update. This is the discrete statement of a frozen hydrodynamic background.

The scalar advection operator is then
\begin{equation}
F_{q_n}^{\rm adv} = s_n^{\rm up} F_\rho,
\end{equation}
with an optional explicit diffusive contribution
\begin{equation}
F_{q_n}^{\rm diff} = -\kappa \rho_f \nabla s_n.
\end{equation}
The total scalar flux is $F_{q_n}=F_{q_n}^{\rm adv}+F_{q_n}^{\rm diff}$. Physically, this means that \SO does \emph{not} define a separate scalar transport solver. It defines a conservative scalar operator built on top of the hydro face mass flux, while suppressing the built-in evolution of the hydro conserved state itself.

\subsection{Why this is not kinematic evolution}
The ambiguity arises because \kin also uses a velocity-only CFL branch. That superficial similarity is not enough. In non-relativistic hydro, \code{time/evolution=kinematic} restricts the solver choice to \code{advect}, but the density and momentum equations are still advanced. The hydro state therefore continues to evolve under the discrete mass-flux divergence generated by the advection solver. A continuum statement such as $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}=0$ does not by itself imply that a discrete upwind density update vanishes identically. Under \SO, the built-in hydro update does vanish identically. Under \kin, it does not.

This distinction is more than bookkeeping. For scalar-mixing studies the prescribed-velocity problem is only clean if the code itself does not alter the hydrodynamic background through built-in update paths. That is the problem solved by \SO. The present scalar-mixing verification suite confirms that behavior: the hydro state remains fixed to machine precision while the passive scalar evolves under advection and diffusion. We return to that verification in Section~\ref{sec:validation}.

\section{Common Spectral Framework}
\label{sec:spectral-framework}
The second uncertainty concerns the meaning of the user-specified turbulent spectrum. AthenaK now interprets the relevant input parameters in terms of the \emph{velocity field} irrespective of whether the field is generated by direct projection or by stream potentials. The common target is a shell-integrated velocity spectrum
\begin{equation}
E_u(K) \propto K^{-\mathrm{expo}},
\label{eq:target-spectrum}
\end{equation}
within the driving band
\begin{equation}
K \in \left[\code{turb\_nlow},\,\code{turb\_nhigh}\right].
\end{equation}
Here $K$ denotes the integer shell index associated with the dimensionless mode number $|\mathbf{n}|$.

We write the real initialized field as a retained half-space sum
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{\mathbf{n}\in\mathcal{H}_{\rm kept}}
\left[\mathbf{a}_{\mathbf{n}}\cos\left(\mathbf{k}_{\mathbf{n}}\cdot\mathbf{x}\right)
- \mathbf{b}_{\mathbf{n}}\sin\left(\mathbf{k}_{\mathbf{n}}\cdot\mathbf{x}\right)\right].
\label{eq:real-series}
\end{equation}
The half-space $\mathcal{H}_{\rm kept}$ contains exactly one representative of each implicit complex-conjugate pair so that the initialized field is real by construction. The isotropic shell index is
\begin{equation}
K = \left\lceil |\mathbf{n}| - 10^{-12}\right\rceil,
\end{equation}
and the corresponding physical wave vector is $\mathbf{k}_{\mathbf{n}} = 2\pi (n_x/L_x,n_y/L_y,n_z/L_z)$.

The retained catalog is produced in two steps. First, AthenaK enumerates every admissible half-space mode in the band of interest. Second, it performs importance sampling above the user scale \code{turb\_k\_crit}. The acceptance probability is
\begin{equation}
p_{\rm acc}(k) =
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
1, & k < k_{\rm crit}, \\
\left(k_{\rm crit}/k\right)^2, & k \ge k_{\rm crit},
\end{array}
\right.
\label{eq:importance-sampling}
\end{equation}
and each retained mode is multiplied by $p_{\rm acc}^{-1/2}$ so that the shell-integrated variance remains unbiased. This matters primarily in 3D, where the full mode count grows quickly with $k_{\max}$.

Once the coefficients are synthesized, AthenaK removes any residual volume-averaged velocity and applies a uniform rescaling so that the final field satisfies the requested rms speed \code{turb\_v\_rms}. The meaning of the input parameters is therefore common across all methods:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \code{turb\_nlow} and \code{turb\_nhigh} define the \emph{velocity} band;
\item \code{turb\_expo} defines the target shell-integrated velocity slope in that band;
\item \code{turb\_v\_rms} fixes the final global rms speed after initialization;
\item \code{turb\_k\_crit} controls retained-mode importance sampling, not the physical driving band.
\end{enumerate}

To make those choices reproducible, the problem generator now accepts
\begin{equation}
\code{problem/turb\_dump\_generator\_diagnostics} = \code{false}
\end{equation}
by default. When enabled, AthenaK writes a sidecar JSON file \code{basename.turb\_init\_diag.json} containing the retained mode catalogs, coefficients, shell spectra, and latent stream fields required to reproduce the figures below.

\section{Two-Dimensional Velocity Generators}
\label{sec:2d}
The two-dimensional problem resolves a simpler uncertainty: how should a target velocity spectrum be realized when one can either project a random vector field or differentiate a stream function? The answer is that both routes are possible, but their coefficient scalings differ because the stream function carries one extra power of $k^{-1}$ relative to the velocity.

\subsection{Projection in 2D}
In 2D projection mode AthenaK first draws Gaussian vector coefficients in the plane and then removes the component parallel to $\mathbf{k}$. For a retained mode with isotropic magnitude $k$ the projected coefficient must scale as
\begin{equation}
|\mathbf{u}_{\mathbf{k}}| \propto k^{-(\mathrm{expo}+1)/2}
\label{eq:2d-projection-law}
\end{equation}
in order for the shell-integrated velocity spectrum to follow Equation~(\ref{eq:target-spectrum}). The extra $+1$ in the exponent is the shell-count correction appropriate to two dimensions, where the number of available modes per shell grows linearly with $k$.

This construction is spectrally direct: the stored coefficients are already velocity coefficients. It is therefore the simplest route when the user wants a prescribed 2D solenoidal velocity field but does not need an underlying scalar potential. Because the initialized field is built mode by mode and then projected, \code{turb\_sol\_frac} remains meaningful in projection mode.

\subsection{Stream function in 2D}
In 2D stream mode AthenaK instead initializes a scalar stream function
\begin{equation}
\psi(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{\mathbf{n}\in\mathcal{H}_{\rm kept}}
\left[a_{\mathbf{n}}^\psi \cos\left(\mathbf{k}_{\mathbf{n}}\cdot\mathbf{x}\right)
- b_{\mathbf{n}}^\psi \sin\left(\mathbf{k}_{\mathbf{n}}\cdot\mathbf{x}\right)\right]
\end{equation}
and defines the velocity by
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{u} = \left(\partial_y\psi,\,-\partial_x\psi,\,0\right).
\label{eq:2d-stream}
\end{equation}
Because each derivative contributes one power of $k$, the stream-function coefficients must satisfy
\begin{equation}
|\psi_{\mathbf{k}}| \propto k^{-(\mathrm{expo}+3)/2}.
\label{eq:2d-psi-law}
\end{equation}
This extra factor of $k^{-1}$ is the entire point of the stream construction: the user controls the \emph{velocity} spectrum while AthenaK determines the compatible stream-function spectrum automatically.

Figure~\ref{fig:methods-2d} shows both 2D generators for the common reference setup $(512^2,\ k_{\min}=2,\ k_{\max}=128,\ \mathrm{expo}=5/3)$. The projection and stream realizations differ visibly in their latent representation but not in their intended velocity-band control. The stream-function map is smooth at large scales because $\psi$ carries the additional low-pass weighting implied by Equation~(\ref{eq:2d-psi-law}), while the velocity field retains the target $-5/3$ shell slope.

\section{Three-Dimensional Velocity Generators}
\label{sec:3d}
The three-dimensional problem is subtler because the stream construction is nonlinear. A 3D projection field is still a linear projection of Gaussian vector coefficients. A 3D stream field is a Clebsch product of two independently synthesized scalar potentials. The uncertainty is therefore no longer just an exponent shift. One must determine which shell weights of $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$ realize the requested \emph{velocity} spectrum after the cross product is taken.

\subsection{Projection in 3D}
The 3D projection construction is the direct generalization of the 2D method. Random vector coefficients are drawn in three dimensions, projected perpendicular to $\mathbf{k}$, and then synthesized. Because the shell occupancy now grows as $k^2$, the appropriate coefficient law is
\begin{equation}
|\mathbf{u}_{\mathbf{k}}| \propto k^{-(\mathrm{expo}+2)/2}.
\label{eq:3d-projection-law}
\end{equation}
This is the same amplitude law AthenaK already used in 3D projection mode; the present implementation retains it.

\subsection{Clebsch stream construction in 3D}
In 3D stream mode the initialized field is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{u} = \nabla \phi_1 \times \nabla \phi_2,
\label{eq:clebsch}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$ are two independent scalar potentials. In Fourier space the retained velocity coefficients satisfy
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{r}) =
-\sum_{\mathbf{p}+\mathbf{q}=\mathbf{r}}
\left(\mathbf{p}\times\mathbf{q}\right)
\hat{\phi}_1(\mathbf{p})\hat{\phi}_2(\mathbf{q}).
\label{eq:clebsch-convolution}
\end{equation}
Equation~(\ref{eq:clebsch-convolution}) shows immediately why the 3D stream problem differs from the linear projection problem. The velocity spectrum is now a quadratic functional of the potential coefficients.

AthenaK no longer tries to invert this quadratic map through a separate
response-tensor fit. Instead the user specifies
\code{problem/turb\_velocity\_method=clebsch} and a desired velocity roughness
parameter \code{problem/turb\_alpha}. The code converts that to
\begin{equation}
s_\phi =
\begin{cases}
3 + 2\alpha, & \alpha \ge 0,\\
3 + \alpha, & \alpha < 0,
\end{cases}
\qquad
s_u = 2\alpha + 1,
\end{equation}
draws Gaussian scalar coefficients on the retained mode catalog, rescales each
retained shell so that $E_{\phi_1}(k)$ and $E_{\phi_2}(k)$ are exact
$k^{-s_\phi}$ laws over the accepted shell band, forms the retained Clebsch
velocity coefficients, and finally normalizes the realized field to zero mean
and the requested rms speed.

Two points are important physically. First, the latent scalar spectra are now
explicit auxiliary objects rather than fitted ones. Second, the resulting
velocity slope remains only approximate in practice, because the Clebsch map is
quadratic and the 3D retained mode catalog is importance-sampled through
\code{turb\_k\_crit}. Figures~\ref{fig:methods-3d}--\ref{fig:clebsch3d-seed-audit}
summarize the resulting 3D behavior.

\section{Validation and Diagnostics}
\label{sec:validation}
The figure campaign used to validate the present implementation adopts a single reference setup so that the method comparison is not obscured by varying resolution or forcing parameters. The 2D cases use a $512^2$ domain with \code{turb\_k\_crit}=128. The 3D cases use a $256^3$ domain with \code{turb\_k\_crit}=2. All four runs share
\begin{equation}
\code{turb\_nlow}=2,\qquad
\code{turb\_nhigh}=128,\qquad
\code{turb\_expo}=5/3,\qquad
\code{turb\_v\_rms}=1,
\end{equation}
and a common random seed. Figure~\ref{fig:overview} provides the high-level comparison.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/scalar_mixing_velocity_methods/overview.png}
\caption{Overview of the four implemented velocity generators. The left block
shows representative 2D slices of the initialized speed field for 2D
projection, 2D stream, 3D projection, and 3D Clebsch. The right panel shows the
corresponding shape-normalized velocity spectra together with the target
$k^{-5/3}$ law. The titles report the measured initialization time and the
spectral divergence norm for each case.}
\label{fig:overview}
\end{figure*}

The initialized fields satisfy the requested rms normalization to better than $10^{-10}$ in all four runs. The spectral divergence norm,
\begin{equation}
\left[\frac{\sum_{\mathbf{k}} |\mathbf{k}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}|^2}
{\sum_{\mathbf{k}} k^2 |\hat{\mathbf{u}}|^2}\right]^{1/2},
\end{equation}
remains very small in all four cases. The projection and 2D stream generators
follow the requested $-5/3$ law closely. The representative 3D Clebsch
realization is rougher, which is visible in Figures~\ref{fig:overview} and
\ref{fig:clebsch3d-realization}, but that roughness is now easy to interpret:
the retained scalar inputs are exact shell laws, while the velocity field is an
approximate quadratic consequence of those inputs.

Because the 3D Clebsch construction is stochastic, the single-seed figure
campaign is supplemented by the multi-seed audit in
Figure~\ref{fig:clebsch3d-seed-audit}. This is the stronger validation result:
the current implementation is not merely producing one good-looking
realization, it is producing a repeatable band-limited Clebsch field whose
retained slopes and leakage remain controlled across seeds.

The diagnostics export itself is accurate enough to support a figure-first workflow. For the projection cases, the shell spectra reconstructed from the dumped velocity coefficients agree with the FFT-based spectra of the initialized field at the level of a few parts in $10^{-9}$ in the log-rms sense over $k=2$--$128$. For the stream cases, the latent fields dumped in the JSON sidecar reproduce independently reconstructed slices to machine precision: the maximum absolute reconstruction error is $6.2\times10^{-16}$ for the 2D $\psi$ slice, $2.6\times10^{-15}$ for the 3D $\phi_1$ slice, and $3.4\times10^{-15}$ for the 3D $\phi_2$ slice. This matters because the stream potentials are not present in the standard hydro output; without the sidecar they could only be inferred indirectly.

The transport operator itself is validated independently of the velocity-generator comparison by the dedicated \code{scalar\_mixing\_test} benchmark. In that problem AthenaK advects and diffuses a passive-scalar blob in a uniform prescribed velocity field while the hydro state remains fixed. Figure~\ref{fig:transport-verification} summarizes the result: the initialized hydro background is preserved, while the scalar evolution converges toward the analytic advection-diffusion solution. This is the correct discrete problem for prescribed-velocity scalar mixing, and it is the hydro-side foundation on which the velocity-generator study rests.

\section{Discussion and Practical Guidance}
\label{sec:discussion}
The results above support four practical conclusions.

First, \SO is the correct mechanism for a frozen-background scalar problem. It retains the conservative scalar flux tied to the hydro face mass flux, but it does not evolve the hydro conserved variables through built-in update paths. \kin is a different numerical problem and should not be used as a synonym for prescribed-velocity scalar transport.

Second, projection mode is the most direct route when the primary objective is faithful control of the initialized velocity spectrum. In both 2D and 3D it maps the user inputs almost transparently onto velocity coefficients, and in the present reference campaign it recovers the requested $-5/3$ slope with the smallest ambiguity.

Third, stream mode should be chosen when the latent scalar potential is
physically or diagnostically important. In 2D that latent object is the
familiar stream function $\psi$, which yields a particularly clean relation
between the velocity and vorticity structure. In 3D the latent objects are the
Clebsch potentials $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$, whose retained shell spectra are now
set directly from the requested velocity roughness parameter.

Fourth, the 3D Clebsch construction should still be understood as an
approximate mapping from user slope to realized velocity slope. AthenaK no
longer hides that fact behind a separate shell-fit optimizer. It instead makes
the retained scalar inputs explicit, forms the Clebsch product directly, and
lets the residual finite-sample roughness appear in the realized velocity
spectrum.

The implementation is now in a regime where the remaining tradeoffs are interpretable. The hydro-side semantics are clear. The common spectral inputs mean the same thing across all four modes. The latent fields can be inspected directly. That is a substantially better basis for prescribed-velocity scalar-mixing studies than the combination of ambiguous frozen-hydro semantics and opaque stream-function initialization that existed before this rewrite.
